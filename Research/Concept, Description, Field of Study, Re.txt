Density of States: The density of states function, W(E), describes the number of states per unit energy interval available to a system. The given formula is for a three-dimensional system, where n is the particle density, kB is the Boltzmann constant, m is the mass of the particle, h̄ is the reduced Planck's constant, e is the elementary charge, and c is the speed of light.
Spatial Correlation Function: The spatial correlation function, G(r), describes the spatial correlations in a system. It is defined as the ensemble average of the product of the wave function and its Hermitian conjugate at two different points in space, r and r+r′. The angular brackets denote the ensemble average.
Operators for the Wave Function and its Hermitian Conjugate: In quantum mechanics, the wave function, Ψ, describes the state of a system. The Hermitian conjugate of the wave function, Ψ†, is used to calculate the probability density of finding a particle at a particular point in space.
Spatial Fourier Transform: The spatial Fourier transform, G(q), is a mathematical operation that transforms a function from real space to reciprocal space. It is used to analyze the spatial correlations in a system. The given formula is for a three-dimensional system, where q is the wave vector, r is the position vector, and i is the imaginary unit.
Probabilistic Interpretation and Ensemble Average: In quantum mechanics, the square of the absolute value of the wave function, |Ψ|², is interpreted as the probability density of finding a particle at a particular point in space. The ensemble average is used to calculate the average value of an observable over many realizations of the system.
Phase Coherence: Phase coherence is a measure of the correlation between the phases of two waves. The given formula describes the spatial correlation function, g(rc), for a system with phase coherence. The subscripts i and j denote two different points in space, and rc is the correlation length.
Statistical Mechanics: Statistical mechanics is a branch of physics that uses statistical methods to describe the behavior of large systems of particles. It is used to derive the thermodynamic properties of a system from the microscopic behavior of its constituent particles.
Thermodynamic State Variables: Thermodynamic state variables are quantities that describe the state of a system. The given list includes pressure (p), volume (V), temperature (T), internal energy (U), Helmholtz free energy (F), Gibbs free energy (G), and entropy (S).
Microstates: A microstate is a specific configuration of the particles in a system. The number of microstates, N, describes the number of ways that the particles can be arranged.
Total Number of Particles: The total number of particles, Np, is the number of particles in the system.
Number of Spin States per Particle: The number of spin states per particle, ns, is the number of possible spin orientations for each particle.
Degeneracy: Degeneracy, deg(N,Np,ns), is the number of microstates that correspond to the same macroscopic state. It is a function of the number of microstates, the total number of particles, and the number of spin states per particle.

A single isolated microtubule segment exhibits no measurable mechanical response when subjected to external forces due to its high rigidity.
However if two adjacent segments are linked together via weak bonds they exhibit anomalous viscoelasticity - exhibiting elastic responses over short timescales followed by viscous flow over longer timescales.
This effect arises because individual tubulin proteins making up each segment have discrete vibrational modes corresponding roughly to phonon quanta associated with sound waves.
When two segments are joined together their collective motion becomes correlated leading them out-of-phase along one axis but in-phase along another axis resulting in net zero force applied back onto the surrounding medium.
